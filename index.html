Integral (area under the error curve)

The integral is the total area under the error curve over time. The smooth purple shading is the true continuous integral. The orange rectangles are what the discrete-time PID actually computes — one rectangle per sample, height equal to the error at that sample, width equal to the sample period. The chunkier the rectangles, the worse the approximation.

Derivative (slope of the error curve)

The derivative is the slope of the error curve at any moment. The thin grey line is the true tangent at the marker. The orange line is what the discrete-time PID actually computes — the slope between two consecutive samples. At high sample rates the two are almost the same. At low sample rates the orange line can disagree wildly with the true slope.

What to try

Start here
Move the slider all the way to the right (200 ms = 5 Hz). The orange rectangles miss huge chunks of the curve. Now move it back to 1 ms (1000 Hz). The rectangles match the curve almost perfectly. The WonderBot's PID runs at 10 ms (100 Hz). Find that value and see whether the approximation is good enough.

Why this matters

Faster sampling = better approximation, but more CPU time spent. Slower sampling = saves CPU, but the integral and derivative the PID sees no longer match what the maths textbook expects. Engineers pick a sample rate that is fast enough for the dynamics of the system but no faster.
